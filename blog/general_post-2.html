Understanding Structural Analyses in FEM - Part 2: Linear vs. Nonlinear Analysis

Welcome back to our journey through structural analysis in the world of Finite Element Method (FEM). In this second installment, we delve into a critical distinction: linear vs. nonlinear analyses. Understanding this differentiation is essential for solving complex engineering problems.

In our previous post, we explored the difference between static and dynamic analyses, setting the stage for a more profound understanding of structural analysis in FEM. Now, we turn our focus to the question of linearity, a fundamental aspect that significantly impacts how we approach structural problems.

Linear Analysis: Linear analysis assumes that the behavior of materials and structures is governed by linear relationship between forces and displacements.

  • Proportionality: Stress and strain are directly proportional. The material behaves elastically within its linear range.
  • Stiffness matrix is constant.
  • Solving process is relatively short.
  • This is used as a first estimate prior performing nonlinear analysis.
  • Superposition: Linear analysis allows for the superposition of effects. This means you can combine the results of different loads or boundary conditions.
  • Linear analysis is valuable for structures that experience relatively small deformations and loads within the linear elastic range of materials.
  • It's efficient and straightforward to apply.

Nonlinear Analysis: Nonlinear analysis acknowledges that real-world behavior often deviates from linearity, especially under large deformations, material nonlinearity, and other complex conditions. The relation between applied forces and displacements is nonlinear.

  • Nonlinear effects can originate from geometric nonlinearity (large deformations), material nonlinearity (elasto-plastic material) and contact.
  • Stiffness matrix is not constant, and hence, a different solving strategy is used.
  • Crudely, a nonlinear system is one whose behavior is not simply the sum of its parts or their multiples. Hence, they are often difficult (or impossible) to model.

There are 3 types of nonlinearities.

  • Geometric Nonlinearity: The structural deformations are considered in formulating the constitutive and equilibrium equations.
    • Large deformation analysis based on geometric nonlinearity: Applications like metal forming, tire analysis, medical device analysis require large deformation nonlinear analysis.
    • Small deformation analysis based on geometric nonlinearity: Applications like analysis involving cables, arches and shells (slender structures undergoing finite displacements and rotations).
    • Note: Deformations over 5% are usually considered as large deformations.

  • Material nonlinearity: Nonlinear behavior of the material based on the current deformation, deformation history, rate of deformation, temperature, pressure, etc.
    • Example: Large strain (visco) elasto-plasticity and hyperelasticity (rubber and plastic materials).
